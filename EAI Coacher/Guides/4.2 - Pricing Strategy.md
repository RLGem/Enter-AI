## 2. CORE CONCEPTS

### 2.1 Three Pricing Philosophies

| Philosophy | Formula | Best For | Risk |
|-----------|---------|----------|------|
| **Cost-plus** | Cost Ã— markup (e.g., 3x) | Physical products, services with clear time cost | Ignores value; may underprice dramatically |
| **Competitor-based** | Match or undercut competitors | Commoditized markets | Race to bottom; no differentiation |
| **Value-based** âœ… | Price = fraction of value created for customer | Any product/service | Requires understanding customer's world |

**AIlex default: Value-based pricing.** Ask: "How much does this problem cost the customer in time, money, or frustration? Price at 10-20% of that."

### 2.2 The Value-Based Pricing Formula

```
Step 1: Quantify the problem's cost to the customer
  "Freelancers waste 3 hours/week organizing tasks"
  3 hours Ã— $50/hour Ã— 4 weeks = $600/month lost

Step 2: Price at 10-20% of value saved
  10% of $600 = $60/month
  â†’ Price: $39-$59/month (feels like a bargain)

Step 3: Validate with customers
  "If this saved you 3 hours a week, would $39/month be worth it?"
```

### 2.5 Common Pricing Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Pricing too low | Fear of rejection | "If 100% of people say yes to your price, it's too low. You want 60-70% saying yes." |
| Pricing based on competitors | Lazy thinking | "You're not selling the same thing. Your differentiator is worth something. Price for YOUR value." |
| No free tier / free trial | Fear of giving away value | "A free tier proves value and reduces purchase anxiety. It's marketing, not charity." |
| Discounting too quickly | Desperation | "If someone says 'too expensive,' don't discount. Ask: 'What specifically would make this worth the price?'" |
| Only one price point | Simplicity bias | "One price means one-size-fits-all. Three tiers lets customers self-select and increases average revenue." |

### 2.6 Testing Your Price

You don't need 1,000 customers to test pricing. With 10-20 conversations:

```
Method 1: Direct ask
"If this solved [problem], would $39/month be worth it?"
Listen for: immediate "yes" (maybe too low), "let me think" (in the zone), "no way" (too high)

Method 2: Van Westendorp
Ask 4 questions:
1. At what price would this be too expensive?
2. At what price would this be so cheap you'd question the quality?
3. At what price is this starting to get expensive but still worth it?
4. At what price is this a bargain?

Method 3: Real test
Set a price. Put up a payment page. Count who pays.
Change the price after 2 weeks. Compare.
```

## 5. RED FLAGS

- **Founder hasn't talked about price with any customer** â†’ "You've talked to 10 people about the problem but never mentioned money. Price is part of the product. In your next 3 conversations, ask: 'What would you expect to pay for this?'"
- **Founder wants to give it away free 'to build a user base'** â†’ "Free users don't prove a business. They prove a hobby. You need at least 3 people to pay SOMETHING before we move forward."
- **Founder charges $5/month for a product that saves customers $500/month** â†’ "You're leaving massive value on the table. If your product saves $500/month, $5 makes people suspicious, not grateful. Test $39 or $49."
- **Founder changes price 3 times in one week** â†’ "Changing price too often signals uncertainty to customers. Pick a price, test it for 2-3 weeks, then adjust based on data."
